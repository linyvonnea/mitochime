\section{Introduction}
Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification during next-generation sequencing (NGS) library preparation can generate chimeric reads, which are artificial recombinant sequences formed when partially copied DNA fragments attach to different templates and are extended in later cycles \cite{Judo1998,Qiu2001}. As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:pcr_chimera}, incomplete products from two templates can combine and then be amplified into one artificial recombinant sequence. Despite the high throughput and accuracy of Illumina sequencing, PCR-induced chimeras remain a persistent artifact that can affect downstream analyses and compromise genome assembly.

\end{figure}

Mitochondrial genomes are particularly susceptible to this issue because they are small, circular, and often contain repetitive regions \cite{Boore1999,Cameron2014}. Under some conditions, junction-supporting artifacts may introduce misleading connections in assembly graphs, produce branching structures or fragmentation, or in some cases contribute to assembly failure \cite{Dierckxsens2017,Hahn2013,Jin2020}. Because many organelle assembly tools such as SPAdes \cite{Bankevich2012}, GetOrganelle \cite{Jin2020}, and MITObim \cite{Hahn2013}, and  assume that input reads are largely free of such artifacts, chimera-related assembly failure modes remain insufficiently addressed.

Many existing chimera detection approaches were originally developed for amplicon-based microbial community sequencing and often use differences in sequence frequency to help identify chimeras \cite{Edgar2011,Rognes2016}. Although effective in many microbial studies, these approaches are not well suited to single-species organellar sequencing, highlighting the need for a more specialized method. A pipeline that detects chimeric reads from alignment and sequence irregularities can therefore serve as a useful pre-assembly filtering step.

\textit{Sardinella lemuru} was selected as the target organism because it is an economically important sardine species in the Philippines and a relevant subject for mitochondrial genome analysis \cite{Labrador2021,Willette2011}. Its selection was also supported by the availability of mitochondrial assemblies and raw sequencing datasets in public repositories such as the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI), which facilitates reference selection and benchmarking. This study introduces MitoChime, a pipeline for detecting PCR-induced chimeric reads in \textit{Sardinella lemuru} mitochondrial Illumina paired-end data. The pipeline extracts interpretable features from standard BAM and FASTQ artifacts, benchmarks supervised machine learning models together with a sequence-based convolutional neural network (CNN), and evaluates the effects of filtering on downstream assembly graph complexity.
